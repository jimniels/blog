# Something’s Rotten in the State of macOS Icon Design

This is [an iconic observation](https://mastodon.social/@heliographe_studio/115890819509545391):

> If you put the Apple icons in reverse it looks like the portfolio of someone getting really really good at icon design
> 
> <img src="https://cdn.jim-nielsen.com/blog/2026/rotten-icons-macos-pages.png" width="1000" height="270" alt="" />

This isn’t, however, just the story of Apple’s Creator Studio icons. It’s the unfolding story of icon design across the entire macOS platform.

For example, take a look at some of Apple’s other apps like [iMovie](https://www.macosicongallery.com/apps/408981434/):

<img src="https://cdn.jim-nielsen.com/blog/2026/rotten-icons-macos-imovie.png" width="784" height="176" alt="" data-og-image />

Or [Remote Desktop](https://www.macosicongallery.com/apps/409907375/):

<img src="https://cdn.jim-nielsen.com/blog/2026/rotten-icons-macos-remote-desktop.png" width="632" height="176" alt="" />

Apple sets the standard (and the rules) for how icons look on the Mac. Wherever they go, so goes the ecosystem — and they’re taking the entire ecosystem ~~along~~ down with them.

It’s fast becoming the case that if you put _any_ Mac app’s icons in reverse, it looks like the portfolio of someone getting really, really good at icon design.

Even Microsoft — not exactly a bastion of design — starts to look pretty decent with their icons the further back you go. For example, with [OneNote](https://www.macosicongallery.com/apps/784801555/), the app icon’s progression looks like it went something like this: 

- “I made this with AI”
- “I tried to make the AI one, but by hand myself”
- “I don’t need to be constrained by this squircle”
- “Hey, I’m getting better at this”

<img src="https://cdn.jim-nielsen.com/blog/2026/rotten-icons-macos-one-note.png" width="632" height="176" alt="" />

Some 3rd-party apps continue to fight a good fight, even as Apple’s definition of what an icon should be — or what’s even possible — shrinks all around them.

Everyone’s being put in a ~~box~~ squircle. The imposition is real.

I don’t blame any of the 3rd-party app makers. Their designs have to play by Apple’s rules (or [end up in icon jail](https://bsky.app/profile/jim-nielsen.com/post/3mllufajiik2c)). World-class designers like [Matthew Skiles](https://www.macosicongallery.com/designers/matthew-skiles/) or [The Iconfactory](https://www.macosicongallery.com/designers/the-iconfactory/) are still out there striving for excellence, even as they’re hamstrung by the Mac’s latest rules.

When it comes to icon design on the Mac, the sky is no longer the limit: Apple’s icon design sensibilities are. They set the examples of what world-class icon design should look like, but what do you do when the examples are no longer exemplary?
